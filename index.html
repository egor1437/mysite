The Half-Page That Vanished: Nabokov’s Omission in His Russian Lolita

May 22, 2025 • Literary Trivia

When Vladimir Nabokov first published Lolita in English (1955), he was a consummate stylist, famously fastidious about every nuance of wording and cadence. A decade later, in 1967, he decided to translate his own novel back into Russian, bringing Humbert Humbert’s arch voice into his native tongue. This Russian edition—issued by Phaedra Publishers in New York—was hailed as an extraordinary literary event, but it was never intended to be the final word on the text: Nabokov himself warned that crafting a flawless Russian Lolita would require almost supernatural precision.

For more than thirty years, readers of the Russian Lolita assumed Nabokov’s translation mirrored the English original, word for word. In January 2003, however, a Tula journalist named Aleksandr Svirilin published an article revealing that a crucial paragraph—about half a page long—was missing from the Russian translation. Svirilin compared the printed Russian text to Nabokov’s own manuscript of the translation and discovered that an entire passage—rich in both plot detail and linguistic play—had simply been omitted. Moreover, he noted a comical typo in the published text: the Russian word «веер» (“fan”) appears where Nabokov clearly intended «вечер» (“evening”).

Aleksandr Svirilin’s scoop did not go unanswered. Dmitri Nabokov, Vladimir’s son and literary executor, reportedly verified the omission against his father’s translation manuscript and agreed that the paragraph had indeed been left out during the original editing or typesetting process. He then promised that in the next Russian edition of Lolita the missing half-page would be fully restored—finally giving Russian-language readers access to the text in its author-approved entirety.

Nabokov’s prose is notoriously exacting: each sentence in Lolita teems with puns, echoing sounds, and subtle references. Losing even a few lines can shift shades of meaning, interrupt narrative rhythm, or obscure thematic echoes. For scholars and enthusiasts, the rediscovered passage wasn’t merely a matter of textual completeness—it was a key to understanding how Nabokov intended Humbert’s voice to resonate in Russian, and how that voice carries the novel’s moral and ironic tensions.

Following Dmitri’s assurance, subsequent printings of the Russian Lolita included the restored text, along with a brief editorial note acknowledging the earlier omission and that it derived from “scientific scrupulousness”—Nabokov’s own phrase for his painstaking approach to translation. Today, the story of the “lost half-page” stands as a reminder that even the world’s most meticulous writer can be tripped up by the vagaries of publication—and that dedicated readers and journalists can play a vital role in preserving literary integrity.
